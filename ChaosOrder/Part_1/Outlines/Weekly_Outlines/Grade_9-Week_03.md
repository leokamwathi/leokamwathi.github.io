- Focus: June
- Cast: Dex, Alex, Tracy, Kate, Ms. Vale, Nikole briefly dropping off sketch paper
- Hook: After Founder's Day, Ms. Vale pushes the group to think about presentation, not just sound, and asks for an early visual direction before the next rehearsal block.
- Stakes: Creative control, the band's emotional identity, and whether June can protect the group's authenticity without becoming possessive about the vision.
- Summary: June thought getting the band to exist would be the hard part. This week teaches her that keeping it recognizable on her own terms may be harder. The rehearsal room conversation starts practical and quickly turns emotional because everybody hears the word style differently. Kate understands presentation as survival, Dex understands it as system clarity, Alex treats it like mood, and Tracy cares whether the look feels kind rather than forced. June resists anything that feels too polished or fake, but she also starts to realize that refusing definition is still a kind of definition. Nikole's brief drop-in matters because she quietly gives June permission to think visually without having to defend that impulse as shallow. The scene ends with the group not fully agreeing, but with June reluctantly accepting that the band will need a visible identity as well as an emotional one.
- DC: Medium
- MemoryTag: +visual-identity
- Playtest Outcome: Partial Success. June accepts the need for a visual identity, but the conversation exposes how misaligned the group's instincts still are.

- Location: Waterfall High, library side tables and hallway lockers after lunch
- Focus: Dexter
- Cast: Prita, Martin, Tracy, Finn circling the edges, one irritated librarian
- Hook: Dex tries to optimize rehearsal, communication, and school obligations with a shared planning system, only to find that people resent being managed even when the system works.
- Stakes: Trust, usefulness, leadership legitimacy, and whether Dex can adapt when the correct solution feels wrong to everyone else.
- Summary: This is a good Dex problem because he is not wrong in the practical sense. His system would save time, reduce missed cues, and help the group survive the next few weeks. The problem is that it also makes people feel handled. Martin bristles because his schedule already belongs too much to other people. Tracy sees the value but worries the structure will turn care into duty. Prita understands the elegance of the plan and becomes the only person who can explain to Dex why emotional resistance is not the same thing as irrationality. Finn, predictably, keeps poking at the exact places where Dex feels least socially secure. The scene should end in a partial win: the system is adopted in reduced form, but only after Dex gives up the fantasy that competence automatically earns comfort.
- DC: Hard
- MemoryTag: +schedule-friction
- Playtest Outcome: Partial. The system survives in reduced form, but Dex learns that useful structure can still alienate people.
