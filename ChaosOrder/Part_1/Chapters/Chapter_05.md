# Chapter 5: The Ghost

The first-day hallway breathed like a beast. Lockers slammed in friendly violence; schedules flashed in nervous hands; the floor carried a rumor of wet sneaker squeak from a summer that refused to leave.

"New year, new me," Alex said to the trophy case, [New armor.] and caught his reflection long enough to set the grin. He tugged his hoodie strings even, flipped one earbud off to keep the world, and coasted down the corridor like it belonged to him.

Gabriel popped out of a classroom, backpack unzipped like a comet tail. "Prediction market on which teacher cries before lunch," Gabriel said. "I have five on Mr. Thompson if a recorder shows up."

"Thompson cries at the syllable ‘recorder,’" Alex said. He drummed on the locker vents with his knuckles, a five-beat that almost fooled his pulse into agreeing. [Scan without staring.]

Finn skated his fingers along the handrail like it might grind back. "Meteor 2.0 after school," Finn said. "My elbow healed into a better elbow."

"Proud of your sequel mindset," Alex said. He threw them both a finger-gun and kept moving before either of them asked why his eyes were busy counting exits.

The bell tried to be a command and landed as a suggestion. First period did its small theater; Alex sat in the middle row and laughed in the right places. He delivered a joke on his name so the substitute wouldn’t do it first. [Keep the room balanced.] He doodled a chorus in the margin for a song he wasn’t writing and changed two chords to prove he could.

— — —

By lunch the cafeteria had organized itself into countries. Flags were invisible but seating charts were law. Alex joined a queue that moved at the speed of regret and made the line laugh about it until it forgave him.

"Line’s a crime scene," Alex said to the kid behind him, [Don’t look yet.] and stared very hard at the menu board so his neck wouldn’t betray him.

He turned his tray, and there he was. Jayden at the far table with the volunteers, brightness turned down to day-wear. Hat in his hands, not on his head. Laughing at something small like it mattered.

The eye contact happened the way thunder happens—after you know lightning already chose you.

"Hey," Alex said to no one. [Please be a blur.] He broke the gaze first and the tray jumped in his hands, the carton of milk flirting with gravity.

"Yo, you good?" the kid behind him said, quick palm stabilizing the tray.

"Just practicing my milk acrobatics," Alex said. Smile, tilt, exit. His feet chose the side door before his brain could write objections in full sentences.

The stairwell was quiet the way a held breath is quiet. He put his tray on the step, sat one flight up, and listened to the school’s ribs hum.

"Roof," Alex said, [Run now or talk never.] and took the service stairs two at a time.

The door gave with its familiar stubbornness. The sky had stretched a clean sheet over town. Heat lifted off tar in waves that smelled like summer sweat and band practice.

Alex set the tray down near the vent that coughed warmth year-round. He picked at the sandwich like it had offended him.

"New year, new me," Alex said to the empty air. [New hiding place, same old ghost.] He lay back, shoulder blades against gravel, and let the hum of the building come up through bone.

His phone buzzed. June: cafeteria chaos survival pack status? Alex took a photo of the sky and sent it. June: ah, the classic ‘I lunch with God’ move. Save me a cloud. Alex: two clouds. premium.

He didn’t mention Jayden. [Say it and it’s a shape.]

He counted slow to ten and back down, a trick a therapist in a video had suggested to a boy who looked nothing like him. The trick worked the way umbrellas work in sideways rain.

A gull cut a circle overhead, rude and perfect. Alex sat up and decided to finish the sandwich just to prove he could decide anything.

"First day ritual locked," Alex said, [No cafeteria. Not yet.] and built a drumline on the vent with his fingertips until the beat drowned the memory of the cafeteria’s fluorescent hum.

— — —

English introduced itself in the afternoon like a confident stranger. Mr. Harrington wore a tie that suggested whimsy but assigned a seating chart that suggested control. Alex landed by the window.

"We’ll begin the year with voices," Harrington said, writing the word on the board like it could hold weight. "Not mine. Yours."

Alex wrote voices in the corner of his notebook and then wrote the word ghost next to it even though he didn’t mean to. [Erase that.] He didn’t.

Someone in the back asked about creative work for extra credit. Harrington smiled the way a person smiles when they recognize their younger self and don’t quite forgive him. "All credit here is earned in public."

Public. Cafeteria. The word passed like a shadow. Alex turned his head to the window and watched a gull fail to catch a thermal once, then nail it the second try.

— — —

The courtyard poured students out at the end of the day like marbles from a jar. Alex took the exit by the music room and pinged June a three-word code they understood: roof? later?

June sent a photo of a film canister in a palm. June: later. bring snacks or perish.

"Deal," Alex said, [I can do later.] and put his headphones on to score his walk home with a song that knew how to pretend it wasn’t about anything.

— — —

The O’Connell kitchen lit up like weather. Morning slid in sideways; the radio tried on the news and then changed its mind to oldies. Mrs. O’Connell stacked toast like she was building something that could be defended. Mr. O’Connell did eggs with the care of someone who wrote manuals for a living.

"Curfew exists for a reason," Mrs. O’Connell said, butter moving like discipline.

"It exists to be tested," Mr. O’Connell said without turning, spatula a metronome. "By teenagers specifically."

Finn sat with one knee bouncing to a drum no one else heard. Board leaned against the back door like a co-conspirator.

"We said midnight," Mrs. O’Connell said. "You came in at one ten."

"Fifty-five," Finn said. He bit toast like it might get away. [Don’t joke now.]

"I hear you," Mr. O’Connell said. He slid eggs onto a plate. "And I hear your mother’s heart wearing running shoes."

Finn looked at the plate. The eggs were perfect. His father did eggs the same way every Sunday of Finn’s whole life.

"I lost track," Finn said. He didn’t add: we landed the Boomerang; time stopped; the park was gold; everything felt possible.

"You lose track when you’re twelve," Mrs. O’Connell said, voice trying to be soft and overshooting into brittle. "You’re not twelve."

"I’m fourteen," Finn said, and made it sound like a defense when it was just arithmetic.

Mr. O’Connell set a glass of orange juice by the plate. "We don’t want to pen you in," he said. "We want to know who to call if the board doesn’t bring you home."

The kitchen clock clicked like it was counting opinions. Finn ran a hand through his hair, decided against the hat. "I’ll text," Finn said. [I’ll try.]

Mrs. O’Connell exhaled, long, reeling in the worry like line on a fish she planned to release. "Eat," she said. "Then bike. No skating to school."

"Compromise accepted," Finn said, and made it charming enough that it almost worked.

Mr. O’Connell kissed his wife’s temple with a practiced casualness that telegraphed apology without admitting it. "He’ll be fine," Mr. O’Connell said to the air, which refused to answer.

Finn ate fast and went for his backpack. The board rolled a few inches on its own like it had an opinion.

"Helmet," Mrs. O’Connell said.

"Helmet," Finn repeated, and took it. He hesitated in the doorway long enough to let them see he had heard them, then left in a rush that made the screen door complain.

The radio found a song about summer and refused to let it go. Mrs. O’Connell adjusted the stack of toast one more time. Mr. O’Connell rinsed the pan as if dishes could make a schedule out of a boy.

"He’s good," Mr. O’Connell said, more to himself.

"He’s fast," Mrs. O’Connell said, and watched the yard like speed could be tamed with staring.

— — —

Evening pulled the roof into shadow where it liked to live. Alex brought a bag of chips and two sodas and a joke he’d practice not telling. June arrived with the clatter of a camera bag and the kind of silence that meant she’d chosen to spend it.

"Tribute," Alex said, offering chips. [Say nothing big.]

"Accepted," June said. She sat against the vent and let the metal warm her spine.

They ate noisy food and talked about teachers like they were weather patterns to prepare for. They traded schedules, circled a free lunch in common, and pretended it was strategy, not relief.

"So, the cafeteria—" June said.

"Was a film in the wrong aspect ratio," Alex said, [Do not say his name.] and it was enough for now.

The sky did its color shift while they didn’t look straight at it. Somewhere below, a door clanged and laughter bounced off brick.

"Tomorrow," June said.

"Tomorrow," Alex said. [Roof again.] He tapped the vent twice to set the promise, and the building kept humming under them like it knew how to carry weight.
