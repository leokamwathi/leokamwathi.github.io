# Chapter 1: The Match

"Ugh. Again," Kate said.

Her laptop screen still glowed on the desk like it was proud of itself. The export bar sat frozen at ninety-nine percent, smug. Kate blinked hard, trying to convince her eyes to stop feeling like sandpaper.

She swung her legs off the bed and her tennis bracelet chimed, tiny and bright in the dark. [I did this to myself.]

"Shower," Kate said.

The tile was cold. The water took forever to get warm, then got too warm, then she turned it down like she was negotiating with a faucet. Steam curled around the mirror and made her look softer than she felt.

"Practice before the match," Kate said, because saying it out loud made it real.

She toweled her hair, pulled it into a tight ponytail, and reached automatically for the locket.

"Okay," Kate said, thumb on the clasp.

She didn't open it. She just rubbed the metal until it warmed, then clicked it like a seatbelt and let it sit against her skin. [Don't fall apart in the morning.]

On the kitchen counter, a folded note waited by her empty coffee mug.

Kate knew Grace's handwriting the way she knew her own footwork patterns.

"Emergency," Kate read. Her mouth went tight before she hit the next line.

*Had to go to the facility. I promise I'll be there for your match. Love you.*

"Sure," Kate said.

She folded it smaller than it started, like that could make it less true, and slid it into her bag with her grips and spare strings. [Guess she'll miss this one too.]

Outside, the air still had that early-morning bite that pretended it was fall. The pool house smelled faintly of chlorine even with the door shut, like the place couldn't stop being itself.

"Footwork," Kate said.

She jogged to the courts with her bag bouncing against her hip, irritation tapping time in her ribs. If she could get her legs right, everything else would follow. It always did.

---

"Two games down," Kate said.

The scoreboard didn't argue.

The match was already in progress, and Mackenzie was already calm, already ahead. Mackenzie stood behind the baseline like she belonged there, visor neat, bounce steady, no wasted motion.

"My serve," Kate said.

The ball felt too light in her hand, like it could betray her on purpose. She bounced it once, twice, then tossed.

The first serve clipped the tape and died.

"Fault," the umpire said.

Kate caught the second ball from the ball kid and didn't look up. [Not today.]

"Reset," Kate said.

Second serve. Less ego. More percentage. The ball cleared clean, landed deep, and Mackenzie's return sat up a little.

Kate stepped in.

"Short," Kate said, and the word came out like a trigger.

She drove it crosscourt.

Mackenzie slid, got there early anyway, and lifted a high slice back that floated just long enough to make Kate feel like she had time. The crowd noise thinned into a hush that meant people were watching now.

"Good," Kate said.

She moved her feet. Split-step. Load. Hit. Recovery. The rhythm clicked into place and the point belonged to her.

Applause came fast, a quick scatter of hands, lighter than before.

Kate let herself breathe once. [I can do it. Footwork will win.]

Mackenzie walked to the baseline, tapped her strings, and glanced up like she was checking the weather.

"Nice," Mackenzie said.

Kate hated that it sounded genuine.

The next rally went longer.

Thwack.

Skid.

Squeak.

Kate chased a wide ball, got it back, then got sucked forward on the next one. She reached instead of moving. Her forehand went for the line.

It missed by an inch.

"Out," the line judge said.

Kate's mouth went dry.

Mackenzie didn't celebrate. She just reset faster.

"Ready?" Mackenzie said.

"Always," Kate said.

Mackenzie served. Clean. Heavy. Kate got a racquet on it, but the return landed short again.

Mackenzie stepped in and punished it.

The applause returned, late and loud, like the crowd was catching up to what had already happened.

Kate tried to stay inside her feet. She tried to make her legs obey.

Mackenzie didn't let her.

"Game, set, match," the umpire said.

For a second, Kate couldn't tell if the heat on her face was sun or humiliation.

At the net, Mackenzie leaned in and tapped racquets. "Good game." Her smile was small, controlled.

"Yeah," Kate said, smile locked. [It was just a game.]

Mackenzie's eyes held for a beat, like she wanted to say something else.

"See you at regionals," Mackenzie said.

"Sure," Kate said. [I just need to work harder next time.]

---

"Five minutes," Kate said.

The locker room smelled like disinfectant and wet grip tape. Her shoes squeaked on the tile like it was judging her.

She dropped her bag, leaned over the sink, and let cold water run until her palms went numb.

"Okay," Kate said to her reflection.

Her hands found the locket without looking.

She opened it fast, like stealing.

"I miss you," Kate whispered.

Then she snapped it shut as the door clicked.

"Hey," a voice said.

Mackenzie.

Mackenzie stood in the doorway with a towel in one hand and too much ease in her posture for someone who'd just won. "Tough one." She held the towel out like it was normal. Like they were friends.

Kate took it because making a scene would be worse. She pressed it to her face, breathed through it once, and handed it back.

"Thanks," Kate said.

Mackenzie's mouth twitched, like she knew exactly what that tone meant.

"You played better in the second set," Mackenzie said. "Your feet got… loud." She said it like a compliment.

"Cool," Kate said. [Don't let her in.]

Mackenzie laughed under her breath. "Okay, sorry. That sounded weird. I mean, you locked in." She adjusted the strap of her bag. "Anyway. Regionals." Her eyes flicked to Kate's collarbone for half a second, then away.

Outside, a woman called, "Kenzie!" sharp and impatient.

Mackenzie flinched, just a little.

"That's my mom," Mackenzie said, already moving. "See you." She aimed a grin back at Kate, brighter now that she was halfway gone.

"Yeah," Kate said.

Mackenzie slipped out.

A beat later, Tracy appeared in the hallway, carrying a box tied with a ribbon like she was walking into a birthday party instead of a tennis center.

Mackenzie passed her.

"Hi," Tracy said, automatic.

"Hey," Mackenzie said back, and then she was out the door.

Tracy's eyes followed her for a second, then landed on Kate.

"Don't ask," Kate said.

"Wasn't going to," Tracy said, and stepped closer. "Are you alive?"

"Barely," Kate said.

---

"Quick update," Kate said.

She held her phone up in front of her at arm's length, found the angle that made her look like she had slept, and smoothed her ponytail like it was a personality trait.

"Not my day," she said, voice bright. "Mackenzie played solid. I had a rough start, but I found my feet. We work, we move. Practice later."

She smiled on cue and tapped save.

The second the screen went dark, her shoulders dropped.

"Perform," Kate said, like an insult.

"Hey," Tracy said.

Kate turned.

Tracy lifted the ribboned box a little. "Peace offering. Also bribery."

"You brought a present to my loss?" Kate said.

"I brought emotional support carbohydrates," Tracy said. "Different category." She opened the lid.

Inside sat muffins, warm enough to fog the plastic sleeve. Pineapple. Sugar crust. That exact smell that always got Kate before she could pretend she didn't care.

"Pineapple Muffin Delight," Kate said, and her voice betrayed her.

Tracy's smile softened. "Your favorite."

Kate held out for two seconds, then took one.

"This is unfair," Kate said, biting in.

The top cracked under her teeth, sweet and sharp at the same time.

"You're welcome," Tracy said.

Kate chewed, eyes on the floor, and felt her throat loosen.

"Thanks," Kate said. [Don't cry in public.]

Tracy bumped her shoulder with hers, gentle. "Come back to the pool house later? We can be dramatic and eat baked goods."

"Fine," Kate said.

---

"Beam set," Melissa said.

She lifted onto releve, toes pointed, arms steady. Chalk dust clung to her fingertips like glitter that didn't want to be cute.

"Again," Isabella said from the side. Her clap was sharp. "Light hands."

Melissa landed, heels soft. "One more," she said, breathless.

Martin stood near the doorway with his phone half-raised like he couldn't help himself.

"Don't post that," Melissa said.

"I'm sending it to Joseph," Martin said. "He deserves to see you being terrifying."

Melissa rolled her eyes, but her smile broke through.

Isabella stepped in, adjusted Melissa's shoulder, and pulled back. "Strong core," she said, quieter now. "Gentle landing."

Melissa nodded like that was a spell.

When she stuck the next pass, Isabella's high-five snapped loud in the gym.

"Yes," Melissa said, grinning.

Martin caught it all.

---

"You live in a spa," Tracy said.

Kate kicked the pool house door shut with her heel and dropped her bag like it had wronged her. The late light turned the water outside into a sheet of glass. Chlorine hung in the air even with the windows cracked.

"It's not a spa," Kate said.

Tracy sat cross-legged on the couch like she'd always belonged there. She pulled another muffin from the box and held it up. "It kind of is."

Kate flopped onto the chair opposite her.

"I'm mad," Kate said.

"At Mackenzie?" Tracy asked.

"At everyone," Kate said. "At me." She picked at the muffin wrapper, shredding it into thin strips.

Tracy didn't rush her. She just waited in that annoying way she had, like patience was a hobby.

Kate's fingers found the locket again.

She didn't open it.

"She said I got loud," Kate said.

Tracy snorted. "Your feet?"

"My feet," Kate said.

A knock hit the door twice.

"If that's my mother, I'm going to combust," Kate said.

She yanked the door open.

Martin stood there with his hair still damp like he'd showered too fast, gym bag slung over one shoulder, grin already loaded.

"Hey, Champion," Martin said.

"Don't," Kate said.

Martin blinked, then softened it into something more careful. "Okay." He stepped inside anyway. "I brought… nothing." He patted his empty hands like they were evidence.

Tracy leaned forward, stage-whispering, "He brought vibes."

"Bad ones," Kate said.

Martin dropped onto the arm of the couch, making it wobble. "So. Tell me everything. Or tell me nothing. I'm here either way." He looked between them. "What are we hoping for?"

Tracy's eyebrows lifted. "Wow. Deep for you."

"I have layers," Martin said.

Kate stared at him. "No you don't. You have a playlist and a smile and the confidence of a golden retriever."

Martin laughed. "That's still a personality."

"You never take anything seriously," Kate said, and the words came out sharper than she meant.

Martin's grin paused.

Tracy's gaze flicked to Kate, warning.

Kate crossed her arms like that would hold her in place. [Stop swinging at friends.]

Martin cleared his throat, trying to bring it back to light. "I take stuff seriously. Sometimes. Like… Melissa not dying on beam."

"That's not—" Kate started, then stopped.

Tracy broke the silence first. "Okay. Hopes. I'll go." She raised her muffin like a microphone. "I hope Kate stops punishing herself for losing one match."

"Rude," Kate said, but her voice didn't have teeth.

"I hope," Tracy continued, "that Grace shows up when she says she will. And if she can't, I hope Kate stops pretending it doesn't hurt." She swallowed. "That's it."

Kate looked away, jaw tight.

Martin rocked his foot against the couch. "I hope we don't get stuck," he said, quieter than usual. "Like… in the same fights. Same loops." He glanced at Kate. "And I hope you let people sit with you when you're mad."

Kate scoffed because it was easier than answering.

"Your turn," Tracy said.

Kate stared at the water outside.

"I hope," Kate said slowly, "that next time I don't start two games down." She let out a breath that almost sounded like a laugh. "And I hope… I don't have to be perfect on camera to feel like I'm allowed to be seen." [Say it. Own it.]

Tracy's face softened.

Martin nodded once, like he'd heard something real and wasn't going to step on it.

Kate reached for her phone, then stopped.

"Not tonight," Kate said.

She took another bite of muffin, sweet pineapple bright on her tongue, and let the room stay quiet for half a second without trying to fix it.
