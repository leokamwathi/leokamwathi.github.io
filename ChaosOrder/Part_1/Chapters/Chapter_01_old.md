# Chapter 1: The Match

"Not the toss. The footwork." Kate rolled her shoulders once, the grip tacky in her palm, the late–August heat sticking to the back of her neck like cling wrap. The ball rose, a pale coin spinning into the blue. She tracked its seam, exhaled, and snapped through it.

The serve clipped the tape and kissed long.

"Of course," she muttered, under her breath, as the line judge raised a flat hand. One more point to Mackenzie. One more point bleeding into a match that should have been hers.

"Reset," she told herself, bouncing on her toes. Sweat slid down her spine. Her legs felt carved out of glass—sharp, brittle, reflecting every tiny crack.

Mackenzie adjusted her visor, the picture of calm. Not a hair out of place. Not a breath out of rhythm. She had that country club smile again, the one that looked gentle from the stands and smug from six feet away.

"You okay, Kate?" Mackenzie’s voice floated across the baseline, all syrup.

"Peachy." Kate twirled the racquet, hiding the flinch in her wrist. "Play."

The next rally stretched long, then longer, each ball a metronome tick on the hard court. Thwack. Skid. Squeak. Kate drove the forehand deep, watched Mackenzie float a high defensive slice, and stepped in. Her breath paused; the moment opened like a door.

Finish it.

Kate went for the line—too fine, too hungry—and painted air instead of paint. The ball drifted wide, an inch of mockery. The umpire called game, set, match.

The applause came like hail. Spiky. Loud. Detached.

"Great match," Mackenzie said at the net, leaning in as they tapped racquets.

"Sure," Kate said, smile locked in place like stage makeup. It's fine. It's only the one you needed. It's only the one you told yourself proves you're not slipping.

Beyond the court, a few parents stood in the shade. A dad with a cooler. A mom with a visor and a shrill, happy clap. Kate scanned the fence the way you check an empty doorway you know is empty. Nothing. Just a rectangle of heat where Grace should be, phone in hand, pretending to watch while answering some email with W.E.I.R.D. in the signature.

Don't look twice. Looking twice makes it real.

She shook hands with the ref. She waved to the stands. She gathered balls into a neat triangle for the next match like a helpful, orderly ghost.

In the locker room, the air was cool and smelled faintly of lemon cleanser and old socks. The fluorescent lights hummed. Kate sat, then stood, then sat again because stillness felt like failure. She reached for her bag, fingers finding the small velvet pocket, the one that always stuck a little at the corner.

"Two minutes," she whispered to no one, as if bargaining with time.

The locket was cold in her palm at first. It warmed quickly, like it remembered her. She clicked it open. On the left, her mother's smile—sharp cheekbones, perfect lipstick, the glint in the eyes that said she was already thinking three steps ahead. On the right, her father’s arm around both of them, his hand big and sure on Kate’s shoulder. They were wrapped in summer light. The picture had a tiny crease where she’d once folded it wrong in a hurry.

"It was one match," she told the picture. "It doesn't mean anything." The words wobbled and snapped back into shape. "It's just data."

Her throat prickled anyway. A drop landed on the edge of the frame and magnified her mother’s iris for a second, turning it into a small ocean. Kate snapped the locket shut and pressed it to the hollow of her throat until the metal left a crescent imprint on her skin.

Okay. Mask on.

She took out her phone, propped it against a water bottle, and checked the frame. The fluorescent light was unflattering; she tilted the camera until the glow felt intentional—edgy, athletic. She smoothed a flyaway hair behind her ear. Her tennis bracelet jingled softly, that tiny, familiar chime.

"Hey team," she said, smile blooming to life on cue, voice two notches brighter. "Quick post-match check-in! So—today was... an exercise in humility. Love that for me." She let a laugh spill, crisp and practiced. "But honestly? Proud of the hustle. The grind doesn’t always pay off immediately—sometimes it just builds. You know?"

Her reflection in the black screen of a locker door watched her with flat eyes. She kept talking to the lens.

"Shout-out to M for a solid game. We pushed each other. That’s what you want. I’m gonna do some recovery, review tapes, then treat myself to something with frosting because balance." Another laugh. Lighter. Effortless. "Anyway, remember: one loss doesn't define you. It refines you."

She stopped recording. The smile dropped so fast it felt like gravity. She hit post before she could think, because thinking meant maybe deleting, and deleting meant admitting it was for her more than anyone else.

Her phone buzzed immediately—hearts, fire emojis, a comment from a brand account with too many exclamation points. Tracy sent a string of cake emojis. Kate smirked despite herself.

"Frosting," she said aloud, softer now. "Right."

"Stick it," Melissa told herself, the chalk dust fine as snowfall on her palms. The gym smelled like rubber and vanilla lotion, that sweet hint from her mom’s purse that somehow lived in the air. The stereo played a soft pop song someone’s team liked to warm up to.

Isabella stood by the beam, arms crossed in the way that looked strict and felt like a hug. "Eyes up, Mel. Breathe. You’ve got it." Her voice was low, sure, a polished stone in a pocket.

Melissa took the beam as if walking out on a quiet thought. Toes, toes, toes. She flowed through the turns, the leap, the little wave of her arms that made the motion look like a secret dance only she knew. Her landing thudded clean. The slight wobble in her ankle corrected with the smallest whisper of muscle.

"Yes!" Isabella clapped once, the sound sharp and happy. "That’s my girl. Again, while your body remembers what ‘yes’ feels like."

"Again," Melissa echoed, grinning, cheeks pink with the effort. Again felt like being held. Again felt like someone had drawn a circle on the floor and written her name inside it.

At the far wall, a boy in a school soccer hoodie—Martin—pretended not to watch, knee rested on a foam roller, attention snagged and unwilling. His phone buzzed face down. He didn't pick it up.

Isabella touched Melissa’s shoulder lightly. "Water break after this. Then we try the series with the music."

"Okay," Melissa said. She stepped back to the beam. The chalk puffed, a small cloud. Stick it.

—

Back at the courts, the sun had slid into late afternoon, a tired coin melting toward the horizon. Kate cycled through her cooldown stretches while pretending not to hear Mackenzie’s laughter somewhere behind the hydrangeas. Her quads burned in a way that felt righteous and exhausting all at once.

"You good?" A voice. Not Mackenzie. A freshman whose name Kate had decided to learn and never had. The girl held out a bottle of water with a nervous smile.

"Always," Kate said, taking it. The water was cold enough to make her teeth ache. "Thanks. What’s your name again?"

"Lena."

"Lena," Kate repeated, as if trying a new grip. "Footwork first. Always. Remember that."

"Footwork," Lena nodded, like she’d been handed a secret code.

Kate shouldered her bag. The locket’s weight sat just under her collarbone now, a secret coin, warm again. Her phone buzzed in steady, tiny heartbeats. New comments. New follows. New noise. The good kind, the kind that felt like being seen from far away.

She paused by the fence. The rectangle of heat where Grace should be had cooled into a rectangle of shade. Kate pictured the lab’s fluorescent lights, the way they reflected in her mother’s eyes when she wasn’t actually looking at you. She pictured the way Grace always said later like it was a promise that the calendar could keep.

"Later," Kate told the empty air, then shook her head at herself. "Get over it. Work."

Her ride pulled up—Tracy’s family minivan, paint a soft green that always reminded Kate of mint frosting. The passenger window rolled down, and Tracy leaned across, eyes warm, hair in a lazy braid.

"I brought cookies," Tracy said, grinning. "Recovery food. Scientific."

"You are a saint," Kate said, meaning it more than she meant most things she said in a day. She slid in, slammed the door, and let the cool blast of the AC iron the heat off her skin.

Tracy held out a paper bag. Cinnamon and sugar and butter fogged the air. "You okay?"

"Obviously," Kate said, and took a bite. The cookie cracked, then melted. Her shoulders dropped without permission. "I’m fine. Just—annoyed."

"You can be both," Tracy said. "Fine and annoyed."

"Great. I’m a complex heroine," Kate deadpanned, then smirked. "How’s the bakery?"

"Busy. Dad tried a new sourdough. Mom says he’s flirting with the yeast again."

"Gross," Kate laughed, real this time, small and leaking out from the middle instead of coming from the edges.

As the van pulled away, the courts shrank in the side mirror. Kate glanced down at her phone. The video thumbnail showed her smiling, bright and invincible. She tapped it once, watched herself talk, watched the way her eyes flickered at the end, the millisecond where the mask slipped before the algorithm could notice.

She locked the screen and leaned her head back. The locket pressed into her skin, a little half-moon reminder. The bracelet on her wrist chimed as the van bumped over a pothole, a tiny bell.

"Next time," she told the ceiling of the van. "Next time I win."

Tracy didn’t ask who she was talking to. She just reached over and squeezed Kate’s knee once, gentle, like pressing a bruise to tell it you knew it was there.

"Next time," Tracy said. "And we’re getting cupcakes after, regardless."

"Balance," Kate said, eyes closed, finally letting the word mean what it meant.
