# Chapter 3: The Hearth

Morning lit Mariposa Bakery like honey in glass. The bell did its hopeful jingle; warm air lifted cinnamon and butter into the street and carried people inside like a promise.

"Taste check or theft?" Diana said, sliding a tray of conchas to the cooling rack, steam lifting in soft curls.

"Quality control," Tracy said. [Steal joy on purpose.] She pinched off a corner of sugar shell and let it crackle between her teeth.

Arthur pulled espresso, hands steady, apron inked with flour fingerprints. "Latte for Ms. Navarro. Extra love today," Arthur said, and drew a heart in milk that actually looked like a heart.

"She’s going to cry again," Diana said, already grabbing a napkin. "In a good way."

"We sell pastries and minor catharsis," Arthur said. "It’s on the chalkboard, small print."

"Add a croissant emoji," Tracy said, [Keep them laughing.] and boxed six guava turnovers with the precision of a jeweler.

The door opened; the room breathed. A little boy held up three fingers from the line like he was placing a serious order for the number three. Tracy flashed back two, a secret pact to make it quick. She liked the choreography—the tilt of tongs, the crisp of paper, the low thrum of the grinder, the way her parents’ orbit kept everyone warm without smothering them.

"How’s the bazaar table?" Diana said. "Father Lemaire stopped by last night with a spreadsheet that should be illegal."

"We’re delivering at noon," Arthur said. "You two do the cupcakes; I’ll handle coffee for the church folks so nobody sins at the raffle."

"On it," Tracy said. [Make them proud without trying so hard.] She piped buttercream in patient spirals, the rows growing like soft constellations. Her hands found rhythm and forgot to be watched.

— — —

The pool house smelled like chlorine and clean towels and summer that wouldn’t admit it was ending. Tracy set the bakery box on the long table, then rang the bell by the sliding door because Kate insisted the bell meant she had staff.

"Enter, peasant," Kate said from the couch without looking up, a towel turban wrapped like a crown. The humor landed half a beat late. Tracy read the air and sat without touching the box.

"Delivery for Princess of the Deep End," Tracy said. [Don’t tease too hard.] She nudged the lid up an inch so raspberry pink peeked through.

Kate stared past the frosting. "Your mom makes the tops too smooth. It’s obscene."

"Your face is obscene," Tracy said, automatic and soft, a phrase they used when the other needed the world to be normal.

Kate’s laugh broke and then held. "I hate losing, Trac."

"I know," Tracy said. [Let her choose the door.] She waited.

"I keep rerunning the last point," Kate said. "Like the ball could decide different this time." She stared at the ceiling fan, eyes fixed like the blades might be a metronome steady enough to quiet the sting. "Mackenzie was—better. Yesterday."

"Yesterday," Tracy said. She didn’t say the other thing they both knew: that Kate’s mom hadn’t come; that absence had weight.

"I told my camera I’m fine," Kate said, a corner of mouth bending like it hurt. "Like I’m some motivational poster that can move air."

"You can borrow my air," Tracy said. [Take mine.] She slid the box closer. "Or a cupcake. They don’t fix anything, but they bribe the brain into standing up again."

Kate lifted the lid and nodded once like a commander approving a plan. "Raspberry first."

"Correct," Tracy said. She broke one in half, handed Kate the bigger piece, and didn’t pretend not to notice.

The sliding door clicked. Martin stood with a water bottle and a sheepish smile, hair damp from a run.

"I come bearing electrolytes and zero advice," Martin said. He held up the bottle like a peace flag.

"Good," Kate said. "Advice is banned."

"House rules," Tracy said. [Let him stay.] She scooted to make space on the edge of the table.

Martin sat, eyes flicking to the cupcakes and back. "Is this a trap?"

"Yes," Kate said. "You are required to suffer with me via sugar."

"I accept my sentence," Martin said, and reached for a plain vanilla like he thought choosing the simplest would make him less complicit.

"That’s the decoy," Tracy said. "Real joy is second row, raspberry."

Martin swapped mid-reach, played along, and took a bite. Powdered sugar freckled the table. "Obscene tops."

"Told you," Kate said.

For a minute the three of them ate and listened to the filter cycle hum. The quiet didn’t press on Kate; it just existed. Tracy liked that math—how the right people could change the value of a room without saying a word.

"Tomorrow I’m going to the courts," Kate said. "I keep missing my backhand placement by a foot."

"I’ll shag balls," Martin said. "And heckle. Respectfully."

"Bring a chicken suit and we’ll call it even," Kate said, mouth curving. The joke hung like a pledge.

"Careful," Martin said. "I’m very suggestible."

Tracy let the banter build its small bridge. [Keep this trio simple: sugar, air, place to land.] She collected the wrappers, stacked them like petals, and set them aside.

"One more rule," Tracy said. "No rerunning the last point today. We’ll replay it tomorrow on an actual court."

Kate breathed out like the room had granted permission. "Okay. Tomorrow."

— — —

The church lot buzzed brighter by noon. Tracy threaded through tents with a fresh bakery box; Alex and June were already at a table, arguing over nothing like it mattered.

"Mariposa delivery," Tracy said, and set the box down between them.

"Hero," Alex said, hands already hovering.

"Two for the photographers," June said, ruthlessly fair.

Tracy watched them bite in sync and felt the small job land where it was meant to. [Love is logistics.] She tightened the red string around the bow on the next box and went to find the volunteers with clipboards.

— — —

The afternoon at the skatepark slouched toward golden. Heat softened the concrete; the sky did its slow peach thing. Finn dragged the rail two feet toward the quarter pipe because two feet felt like destiny.

"Call it: the Meteor," Finn said, palms chalky, hair refusing to be tamed by the hat hooked on his belt loop.

"It’s a disaster name," Jace said, board under one arm, mouth already framing a grin. "Which is on brand."

"I require witnesses," Finn said, and pointed at Gabriel, who was leaning against the fence pretending to be blasé and failing.

"I’m only here to deliver last rites and record content," Gabriel said, phone up, thumb hovering. "Please wreck beautifully."

Finn eyed the run: roll-in, pop, grind the dragged rail into a tiny death-wobble, land on the sliver of ramp, then sail like a meteor that never learned about gravity.

"You don’t have to do this," Jace said, and then ruined it by adding, "But it would be so sick."

"Thank you for your support," Finn said. [I need the rush today.] He pushed, wheels buzzing. The first pop felt clean; the truck bit the rail; the rail shivered like it would rather be anywhere else. Finn committed anyway.

Halfway through the slide, a screw thought about loyalty and decided against it. The rail sagged. Finn’s board jumped reality. The Meteor became the Pancake.

"No, no, no—" Gabriel said, narrating like that would slow time.

Finn cartwheeled into laughter, a flail of limbs and joy. He landed in a sprawl, breath gone for a second, then found it again and whooped at the sky.

"Ten out of ten," Jace said, already jogging over. "You rotated like a dying star."

"I’m honored to be astrophysics," Finn said from the concrete, grinning like pain and pride shared a couch. He held up a hand; Jace hauled him upright in one motion that was too practiced to be new.

"My dude, your elbow," Gabriel said, zooming the camera like the angle mattered more than blood.

"Battle scar," Finn said, inspecting the scrape that looked worse than it felt. [Prove I showed up.] He wiped it with the hem of his shirt and hissed exactly once.

Jace set the rail back to rights with his sneaker, the way you set a painting back on a nail and pretend it won’t fall again. "Round two?"

Finn looked at the rail. The rail looked back. "Round two," Finn said. "But we call it the Boomerang now."

"Because it comes back to smack you in the face?" Gabriel said.

"Because it returns," Finn said. "Because I do."

The three of them reset the run with the reverence of a ritual no adult would sanction. The sun slid lower; the park caught gold in every scuff. Finn kicked off again, and for one long second the board, the rail, the boy, and the day agreed to cooperate.

He landed. The shout that followed belonged to all three of them.

"I’m putting that on the internet forever," Gabriel said, like a blessing.

"Frame the ugly elbow," Finn said. "It’s my good side."

Jace bumped shoulders with him, the casual kind of tenderness boys learn without admitting it.

[Keep finding ways to fly,] Finn thought, and pushed for another run.
