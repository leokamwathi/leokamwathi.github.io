# Chapter 6: The Slacker

Dexter timed his entrance for maximum average. Not first, not late: perfectly forgettable. The commons churned with first-day adrenaline; he dragged his backpack so the worn strap whisper-squeaked, a small domestic sound that did not invite attention.

"Whole semester strategy," Dexter said under his breath, [Invisible is safe.] and scanned for a route that avoided varsity jackets, loud hugs, and any orbit that emitted obligation.

He had mapped his classes the night before with the ruthless efficiency he reserved for code and circuits, then deleted the map so he wouldn’t look like he cared. Locker: middle row. Combination: muscle memory. Contents: two binders (scuffed on purpose), one calculator (set to degree mode just to irritate any borrowed use), three mechanical pencils (two miscolored), and a paperback novel whose spine crease ratio suggested casual, not obsessed.

He closed the locker soft; slammed doors invited story. Hallway traffic broke into current lines. Dexter stepped sideways to let a trio surge past and nearly collided with a bike wheel that did not belong indoors.

Tracy crouched over the front fork of a powder-blue cruiser, brow knit, bakery logo hoodie half-zipped. A cupcake delivery box sat beside her like it was judging her mechanical skill.

"That’s a cry for help," Alex said, passing by and tossing a grin. He kept moving; he had his own choreography.

"I have it," Tracy said, though the wheel wobbled like it wanted litigation.

Dexter could have kept walking. A path existed. He didn’t take it.

"Your quick release is upside down," Dexter said. He pointed, not touching. "And your brake pad is kissing the tire. Friction is sabotage."

Tracy looked up, surprise flickering into curiosity. "You speak fluent bicycle?"

"Hardware is hardware," Dexter said. [Leave after fix. No rapport.] He knelt, flipped the skewer, loosened, reseated the wheel, tightened until snug, then flicked the lever closed with a practiced economy. He spun the wheel. True enough.

"Pedal revolution achievement unlocked," Dexter said as he stood.

"You just saved me from arriving at homeroom like a tragic mime," Tracy said. "Payment: one cupcake?"

"Sugar destroys calibration cycles," Dexter said automatically. [Take it anyway.] He accepted the small box she produced—a single frosted absurdity—then nodded an exit.

"Name?" Tracy said as he started to move.

"Ghost," Dexter said without turning.

"Not helpful," Tracy said, amused.

"Dexter," he added, a concession that felt like he had handed over a line of code too early in a project.

"Tracy," she said, though he’d heard teachers say it last year. Formal mutual recognition executed.

He slipped into the stream and away. The cupcake weighed more than food, lighter than debt.

— — —

Homeroom performed its roll call theater. Dexter answered, voice level. He set the cupcake carefully in the upper corner of the desk like a fragile device cooling. He took out the scuffed binder and wrote the date with letters that looked convincingly rushed.

Group introductions began. He let two louder personalities define the room’s extroversion quotient and shaped his responses to fall below it. [Under threshold avoids invitation.] When asked about summer he said, "Tuned a project," vague enough to kill follow-ups.

His phone vibrated once in his pocket with a calendar reminder he’d set then forgotten to delete: Practice prototype—lighthouse array. He dismissed it without looking. [Don’t reveal the build yet.] A drawing of a switching circuit lived under his thumb nail, invisible to everyone else.

— — —

Lunch approached like weather. Dexter ran the probability tree: cafeteria (noise, social commitment risk), courtyard (sun glare, unpredictable seating), library (too intentional day one), roof (ideal if unoccupied). Decision executed: roof.

Service stairwell: clear. Hand on the push bar: heartbeat spike negligible. He stepped out and the sky handed him quiet.

Vent, tar, distant gull complaint, the hum of industrial fans syncing with breath. "Perfect," Dexter said, [Keep this untouched.] and sat with his back against the low parapet, legs long, binder open to a blank page he pretended was an assignment so no one could label it solitude.

He ate the cupcake in four methodical bites, cataloging texture and sugar ratio like data. Frosting left a smear; he wiped it with an index finger and cleaned the finger with precision. [Don’t enjoy visible things.] He closed his eyes once, just to confirm the silence held.

The door hinges gave a warning creak. Dexter snapped his posture into neutral.

June emerged first—camera bag, expression that classified him instantly. Alex followed—headphones half-on, grin pre-loaded.

"Territory already claimed," Alex said, mock solemn. "Name your terms, Roof Person."

"This was an experiment in unused space," Dexter said. [Abort? Hold?] He angled the binder so the page looked busy.

June eyed the cupcake container by his shoe—the absence of contents a clue. "We allow scientific trials in the presence of offering," June said. "Cupcake seems partial compliance."

"Cupcake was rescue compensation for a mechanical intervention," Dexter said. He wished the sentence less revealed him.

"Translation: you fixed Tracy’s bike," Alex said. "Good early-year karma play. High-fives from the pastry gods."

"Low cognitive load task," Dexter said. [Stop defending.] He closed the binder softly.

"You planning to vanish all year or just until we memorize your face?" Alex said. He sat cross-legged, producing chips like a magician revealing a dove.

"Invisibility preserves optionality," Dexter said.

June dropped beside the vent, camera strap rearranged into a deliberate barrier. "Optionality is code for fear half the time," June said, tone clinical without cruelty.

Dexter held her gaze a beat longer than comfort then looked at the tar seam near his shoe. [She saw too fast.] "Fear is a data point," Dexter said.

Alex slid the chip bag toward the midpoint like establishing neutral ground. "Rule for this altitude: no essays unless they include snack distribution logic."

Dexter considered decline protocols and discarded them. He took a single chip. Crisp, salt, oil, mundane perfection. He did not take a second. [Stay provisional.] "Snack distribution remains efficient," Dexter said.

A gull shouted overhead like it was judging philosophy. June lifted her camera and shot the bird mid-wing with a reflex that looked like muscle memory. Alex tapped a four-beat rhythm on the vent that failed to be a song only because he insisted it wasn’t.

Silence settled, not the brittle kind that demands patching, the structural kind that can hold three people without groaning.

"Tomorrow," Alex said after a minute, setting a tone like a bookmark.

"Tomorrow," June said.

Dexter inclined his head in a fractional nod that could be interpreted as agreement or muscle twitch. [Return protocol accepted.] He picked up the empty cupcake box, flattened it with precise pressure, and slid it into his backpack like evidence stored.

The gull cut another circle. Dexter traced its path with his eyes, imagining vectors and drag, then let the calculation dissolve before it trapped him back in pure variables.

He stood first. "Calibration complete," Dexter said, and left without hurry.

The door closed behind him. The hum carried. On the landing he let out a breath he hadn’t admitted he was holding. [Not fully invisible. Not dead.] He did not decide if that was loss or gain.

— — —

Prita’s desk at home looked like a supply catalog had agreed to pose. Binders in a spectrum of calm blues, tabs labeled with a handwriting style that balanced clarity and aesthetics, planner open to a weekly spread with color-coded blocks.

"You are staging an office supply revolution," Aki said from the doorway, leaning like he had arrived to witness rather than disturb.

"It reduces friction," Prita said, placing a fine-point pen exactly parallel to the planner’s coil. [Chaos will come anyway.] She filled Monday’s after-school slot with: Math review (light). She added a tiny wave doodle she would understand later.

A stack of printed syllabi sat to her left under a glass paperweight shaped like a small planet. She highlighted key assessment dates in a pale gold that read as importance without shouting; then she transcribed them into the long-term section, cross-referencing previous years’ pacing she had archived last spring.

"You could just—wing it," Aki said, tone teasing but soft.

"Wing structure is an illusion supported by bone," Prita said. "I build bone." She slipped a thin adhesive bookmark into the planner’s semester overview page.

Her phone buzzed with a group chat volley of memes reacting to first-day chaos—photos of lockers decorated like shrines, a dramatized shot of spilled smoothie, someone’s attempt at a motivational quote that misattributed Einstein.

She smiled exactly once, a private permission. "I like order at the start so I can choose disorder later," Prita said. [Control is kindness to future me.] She added: English—voice unit (ask Harrington about extra readings).

Aki stepped in and rotated one binder ninety degrees. "Performance test," Aki said.

"Fail," Prita said, and rotated it back with two fingers.

He laughed. "Do you ever get bored of being serenely prepared?"

"Preparation isn’t serenity," Prita said. "It’s scaffolding. Serenity is optional on top." She wrote a micro goal: Talk to new lab partner without scripting. Then underlined it lightly so it looked less like pressure.

Her mother called from downstairs about dinner timing; Prita answered with a precise affirmative. She stacked the stray sheets by size, closed the planner, and felt the click of magnetic clasp like a system sealing.

She looked once at the window—outside, late summer colored the trees in a way that promised change quietly.

"Ready," Prita said, to the room, to the week, to the version of herself catalogued in tabs. [Let the mess arrive on my terms.] She turned off the desk lamp even though there was still daylight and went downstairs, leaving the workspace in a state that looked effortless by design.
