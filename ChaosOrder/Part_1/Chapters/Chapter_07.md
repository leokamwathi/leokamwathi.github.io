# Chapter 7: The Trio

Day three and the roof had started pretending it knew them. Heat rose gentler; gulls cut wider arcs like they’d agreed not to strafe lunch.

"We need bylaws," Alex said, and flattened a napkin like a charter.

"We need air and non‑interference," June said, and leaned the camera bag into service as a backrest.

Dexter set down a container of grapes aligned in a grid. "We need boundary conditions and an exit protocol," Dexter said. He rotated the lid exactly ninety degrees before removing it.

"Article One: Roof is diplomatic territory. No cafeteria wars spill over," Alex said, writing ROOF in block letters with a pen that wheezed.

"Article Two: Food tax allows sampling but forbids theft," June said, taking a single grape.

"Tax rate variable relative to contribution size," Dexter said, and slid the container inward.

Alex leaned back. "Article Three: No full essays unless someone leaks real feelings. Then essays become mandatory."

"That contradicts brevity," Dexter said. [Rules so feelings don’t leak.] He popped a grape.

"Article Four: If anyone needs silence, silence wins. We don’t patch quiet with noise," June said.

Alex’s grin thinned into respect. "Article Four is queen."

"Monarchy approved," June said, flicking a crumb off her knee.

— — —

Lunch settled around them without announcement. Alex narrated a math teacher’s theatrical sigh; June judged three hallway fashion choices; Dexter summarized a lab safety lecture in nine clipped words.

"Mr. Harrington wants ‘voices,’" Alex said, tearing the napkin into a wobbling flag.

"Voices is code for masks he can grade," June said, tracking a gull mid‑turn.

"Voice is emergent pattern under constraint," Dexter said, then added, "Teachers like patterns they can point at."

Alex framed June with his fingers. "June’s voice: razor film critic meets undercover poet."

"Trite," June said, and framed him back.

Dexter angled his head. "Alex’s voice: adaptive comedic interface with latency buffering."

"Translation: funny guy with emotional RAM?" Alex said, accepting it with a bow.

June tapped the lid. "Dexter’s voice: precision hermit in beta."

Dexter inhaled, held, released. "Prefer modular participant."

"Hermit pending patch," Alex said, tossing him a chip; Dexter caught it smooth.

— — —

June pointed the camera at the vent instead of faces. "Texture study."

"You’ll photograph us eventually," Alex said, balancing a soda can until it failed.

"Consent form in triplicate," June said.

"Photographing people introduces emotional artifacts," Dexter said, watching her framing.

Alex snapped fingers. "Look at that: poet, firmware release, stand‑up routine. Accidental band—press release writes itself."

"I don’t play anything," June said. [I want sound.] Heat brushed the thought.

"I can build things that make noise," Dexter said, eyes on the vent.

"Start with not making the roof hum like a spaceship," Alex said, drumming a five‑beat.

June listened, stored it. "Article Five: no calling it a band yet."

"Deferred nomenclature," Dexter said.

Alex saluted the napkin flag. "Deferred everything until we’re less secretly broken." No one contradicted him; Jun e pinned the flag corner with a lens cap.

— — —

"Why roof?" Alex said after quiet settled.

"Light," June said. "Angles. No fluorescent headache."

"Controlled variable set," Dexter said, watching parking lot patterns.

"No cafeteria cold‑air gun firing darts," Alex said like a joke that wasn’t. [Jayden.] He looked straight up.

Silence held without interrogation.

Dexter tapped twice on the parapet. "Article Six: unasked truths remain valid without interrogation."

"Ratified," June said.

Alex exhaled, lighter. "Ratified."

— — —

A shadow crossed; time nudged. Alex stood first. "Tomorrow," Alex said, pocketing crumpled napkin like scrap talisman.

"Tomorrow," June said, lifting the bag.

"Tomorrow," Dexter said, flattening the chip bag before tossing it. He followed them through the door already sketching potential acoustic improvements he would not yet share.

— — —

Malcolm Lewis ran the curve of the track like it owed him. Heat pressed down; the team had left twenty minutes before.

"Last set," Malcolm said, [Earn it.] and launched 300 meters past comfort.

Breath tore rhythm; arms drove economy. Lane paint, forward pull, rubber tang—world narrowed.

Coach Thompson leaned on the fence. "You don’t have to prove today."

"Didn’t yet," Malcolm said, and surged the straight.

He crossed an imagined finish, decelerated with discipline, walked heart rate down while checking splits.

"Hydrate, then done," Coach said.

Malcolm drank. "Done," Malcolm said, looking at empty lanes like possession. [More tomorrow.] He left when the sun dropped one degree—a private threshold.

The scuffed lane kept his cadence like stored pattern; precision waited for collaborators.
