# Chapter 2: The Relic

"Smile for Father Lemaire," June said, [I’m not in this picture.] as two kids stepped into frame beside the bake-sale table. She lifted the camera, the strap biting her neck. Click. Powdered sugar dusted the air like a cheap snowfall; laughter stuck to her skin like humidity.

"Great turn-out this year," Mr. Adeyemi said from the raffle booth. He waved a roll of tickets like a flag.

"Yeah," June said. She took a step back from the crowd, the choir’s warm-up bleeding into the squeal of the espresso machine. [Too many smiles.]

"June!" Suzan waved from the crafts tent, holding up a lopsided origami crane. "You want this one or the even-worse one?"

"Surprise me," June said. She lifted the camera again, framing a row of jam jars lined in perfect jewel tones. Click. [Looks like they belong.]

"You okay?" Principal Lyu said behind her, tie crooked, hands full of pamphlets.

"Peachy," June said. She pretended to adjust the lens. [I need air.]

"Don’t wander too far," Principal Lyu said, already turning to greet a parishioner.

"I won’t," June said. She slid sideways out of the tent’s breathless heat, past the prayer candles, past the bulletin board with last summer’s mission trip photos. [Breaking promises is easy when no one watches.]

— — —

"Ten minutes," June said to no one, [Run.] as she cut down the alley behind the parish hall and followed the salt to the road that fell toward the water.

The beach opened like a bruise: gray, wide, honest. The wind pushed hair into her mouth; the gulls wrote lazy threats across the sky.

"Just one frame," June said, [Lie.] She lifted the camera and shot the empty pier, the leaning lifeguard chair, the rust-bitered ladder climbing into the light.

A girl sat at the far end of the rocks with a sketchbook in her lap, knees tucked in, hoodie sleeves half-covering her hands. Dark hair, messy in a way that looked unbothered, not careless. She tilted her head at the water like it was speaking.

"Study the horizon," June said, [Don’t look at her.] and aimed near, not at, letting the edge of the girl’s profile fall into the left third. Click. She checked the screen and felt her stomach stutter. The light had made something soft around the girl’s mouth, a curve that wasn’t a smile yet.

"One more for exposure," June said. [Don’t be weird.] She shifted two steps, pretending to adjust the strap, and caught hands, paper, wind-tugged hair, the stubborn line of her jaw. Click.

The girl glanced up at the sound. June dropped her camera like it burned and pretended to be very interested in her own shoes.

"Sorry," June said, [I’m not sorry.] and walked toward the lighthouse because turning back would prove it was about the girl.

— — —

"You again," June said to the lighthouse door, [Don’t break the quiet.] as it gave its familiar creak. The air inside held old salt, metal, and the faint, sweet ghost of someone else’s soap.

The mailbox sat where it always sat—bolted to the inside wall, paint flaking, a little brass flag frozen mid-salute. June brushed her fingers over the cold metal and froze.

"Huh," June said. [Who risks writing to me here?] An envelope rested on the lip like it had grown there. Cream paper. Blue ink. Two words on the front in a hand that looked like patience: Piccola.

"For the caretaker?" June said. [For me.] She slid a finger under the flap and unfolded a single page. The ink bit lightly through in places, like the writer had pressed hard to keep from shaking.

Piccola,

You keep trying to be smaller than the room. Some rooms lie. They want you small so they fit.

Go where your breath is honest. A lighthouse is not shy.

—A.

June read it again. And again. The first time the words felt like a dare. The second time they felt like a hand taking her wrist.

"Piccola," June said, [Who still calls me that?] She tucked the letter back into its envelope and pressed it to the inside of her hoodie like her ribs had asked for it.

She climbed the spiral to the landing and let the town unroll under her—the church lot crowded, the bazaar bright with tents; the main street’s painted signs standing like rowboats; the beach a gray hush. The girl on the rocks had gone or tucked herself into the weather, and June hated that she didn’t know which.

"Go where your breath is honest," June said. [Only here.] She slid the envelope into the pouch in her camera bag where film canisters usually slept.

Her phone buzzed. A text from Alex: u alive or swallowed by hymnals?

"Barely," June typed back. [Save me.] She added: lighthouse. bring snacks or perish.

The wind shoved her hair again; she let it.

— — —

"Back to the saint factory," June said, [Don’t roll your eyes.] as she pushed the lighthouse door closed and walked the road uphill. The bazaar noise grew again—the raffle bell, the choir, a baby’s indignant yell.

"We missed you," Principal Lyu said when she slipped in by the lemonade stand. He looked relieved like he’d found a lost flyer.

"Photo loop," June said. She lifted the camera like proof. [Play along.]

"Grab a plate and sit with us for five minutes, okay?" Principal Lyu said.

"Five," June said. [Two.] She took a paper plate, a cupcake that leaned, and a seat at the edge of a folding table where the shade didn’t quite reach.

"How’s the hustle?" a voice said, and Alex arrived like an earthquake wearing headphones. He dropped into the chair across from her, a grin already halfway to a joke.

"Alive," June said, and slid him the cupcake. "Bribe for rescue."

"Sold," Alex said, and took a theatrical bite. "Is this legal icing?"

"Barely," June said. Her camera bag pressed against her ribs.

"Lighthouse?" Alex said. He tapped the table twice to make a little drumline. "Tell me I get a ghost this time."

Tracy threaded through the crowd with a white bakery box tied with red string. "Mariposa Bakery—cupcake order for the bazaar," Tracy said, and set it on the table.

"Hero," Alex said, already reaching for the bow.

"Quality control," June said, [Feed the shake.] and slipped the knot loose. A dozen frosted crowns stared back, perfect and ridiculous.

"Two for the photographers," Alex said. They each grabbed one before the box could be claimed by adults with clipboards.

"Tell Father Lemaire this counts as a tithe," Tracy said, amused. She passed napkins and watched them take synchronized bites.

"Maybe," June said. She didn’t take her hand off the bag. [Not yet.]

"Legal icing," Alex said, crumbs bright on his grin.

The choir hit a note that made the tents hum. June took a breath and it tasted like sugar and sea.

"Go where your breath is honest," June said under the noise, [Keep this.] and let the bazaar exist around her without needing her to love it.
