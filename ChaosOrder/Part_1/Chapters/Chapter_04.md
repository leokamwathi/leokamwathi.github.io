# Chapter 4: The Legacy

The hospital waiting room tried to smell like lemon and failed. Fluorescent lights hummed at a frequency that made thoughts sharpen into points. A muted sports channel ran highlights of last season’s soccer finals like a taunt.

Luciano paced in a rectangle that had already been carved by other worried parents. The badge on his team jacket flashed with every turn. Isabella sat with a hand over her mouth, eyes clocking every door that opened. Martin kept his cleats in a mesh bag by his feet like they might be a passport if anyone asked for proof of belonging.

"They’re running tests," a nurse said from the doorway, voice steady in the way a metronome is steady. "Vitals look better. Doctor will talk with you in a moment."

"Gracias," Isabella said. She squeezed Martin’s wrist once, quick. He nodded like he knew how to translate that squeeze into calm.

Luciano stopped in front of the TV when a striker in blue bent a perfect shot around a wall. "Textbook," Luciano said, almost to the glass. "You see the angle? The hips. Precision."

"I see it," Martin said. [I see a baseline.] He watched the curve and felt something different in his chest, a geometry his father didn’t mean.

Dr. Patel arrived with a tablet and an expression that balanced facts and comfort. "Joseph had an episode of chest tightness and dizziness at practice. His labs and EKG are reassuring. We'll keep him for observation and schedule a follow-up with cardiology to be thorough. Hydration, rest, and dialing down intensity for a bit."

Luciano straightened like someone had called his name in a stadium. "He can walk?"

"He can walk," Dr. Patel said. "But not run sprints today."

"Tomorrow?" Luciano said.

"We'll let the cardiologist set the timeline," Dr. Patel said. "For now, be gentle."

Isabella stood. "Can we see him?"

"Of course," Dr. Patel said, and led them down a hall where the floor shined like it had been taught manners.

Joseph grinned from the bed the way people grin when they don’t want anyone to worry. "I look great in this gown, right?" Joseph said. "Hospital chic?"

"It suits you," Isabella said. She kissed his forehead and sat in the cheap chair like it was a throne.

Luciano hovered at the foot of the bed. "You scared your mother," Luciano said. "And me."

"Scared myself," Joseph said. "Coach was mid-lecture about endurance and the room decided to do a slow spin."

"Coach was right about endurance," Luciano said. "But the body has limits. We respect limits until we push past them."

Martin took the side with the monitor. The steady blip refused to sync with his breath. "You want water?" Martin said. "I can get water."

"I want a sandwich that tastes like not-hospital," Joseph said. "But water is fine."

"Martin will bring the best water of his life," Isabella said. [Keep moving, mijo.] She handed Martin a crumpled five even though he didn't need it.

In the hallway, the vending machine blinked choices like they were decisions about the future. Martin pressed the button for a bottle and it fell with a hollow victory clunk.

Luciano followed and stood close enough to be a mirror. "You see why we push," Luciano said. "Life does not wait. Tryouts are next week. You're ready."

"Ready," Martin said. [For the wrong field.] He twisted the cap and handed over the water with hands that didn’t shake on the outside.

"The Suarez name means something," Luciano said. "You, Joseph—legacy is not a burden. It's a ladder."

"Ladders can tip," Martin said, too quiet to count. Luciano had already turned back toward 3B, where Joseph made jokes out of air.

— — —

At home, the afternoon light fell into the living room in polite rectangles. The house still smelled faintly of hospital lemon that had followed Isabella's sweater home. Luciano took a call from an assistant coach and stepped out onto the patio to speak in half-whispers that still sounded like orders.

Isabella disappeared into the laundry room and returned with a shoebox that had tennis balls printed on the lid. She set it on the coffee table and took the seat across from Martin without explaining.

"Do you remember these?" Isabella said.

"The box?" Martin said. He tried a smile.

"What's inside," Isabella said. She untied the tired ribbon and lifted the lid like something fragile needed daylight.

Polaroids slid over each other—Isabella at sixteen with a ponytail high enough to deny gravity; a clay court smudged into the edges of the frame; a trophy that looked like it believed in her; a newspaper clipping in Spanish with her name circled twice in purple pen.

Martin touched the corner of one photo and felt static. Isabella at the baseline, knees bent, eyes telling the ball where to go. "You never miss this look," Martin said.

"Sometimes," Isabella said. "But I loved the trying."

"Why did you stop?" Martin said. [Please say money. Please say time. Not me.]

"I didn't stop," Isabella said. "I changed where I put the love."

He nodded, because arguments in this house flared and were forgiven and left char. "I like these," Martin said. [I want this.] He held a photo up to the window. The light put a halo on his mother's shoulder.

"You don't have to be your father's version of the name," Isabella said. She folded the ribbon absentmindedly, a ritual for the hands. "You only have to be yours."

"He wants—" Martin said. He didn't finish. [He wants a player who is like a promise.] He set the photo down carefully in the lid like returning a bird to a branch.

Isabella leaned back. "He wants for you what worked for him. It's how some people love."

"And you?" Martin said.

"I want you to have a breath that belongs to you," Isabella said. "And I want Joseph to keep making bad jokes. And I want dinner to not burn." She stood. "Help with the onions?"

"Yes," Martin said. [Tell me it's okay to want a racket.] He followed her into the kitchen where garlic and oil negotiated peace in a pan.

— — —

That night, after plates and after a check-in call to Joseph where he referenced his gown twice for comedy points, Martin found his way to the garage. The house exhaled into quiet. A tennis racket leaned against the wall behind a box of deflated soccer balls like it had been waiting for someone to acknowledge it.

Martin wrapped his fingers around the grip and the weight made sense in his wrist. He bounced a ball against the string bed once, then again, the thup a private metronome.

"You're going to break something," a voice said from the doorway, not unkind.

Isabella leaned on the frame with a towel over her shoulder. "There's a wall behind the school gym," Isabella said. "At dusk, the light is good and no one bothers you."

"Okay," Martin said. [Meet me there.] He set the racket down like a secret he could pick up later without apology.

— — —

The W.E.I.R.D. lab housed its own midnight. The windows had been designed to perform morning even when there wasn’t any. Machines that did not sleep hummed in patient chorus.

Grace Silchenko scanned three monitors at once. One showed a timelapse of cells arranging themselves into a pattern that looked like a promise; another ran code that logged errors in a polite gray; the third displayed a grant portal with a countdown clock impersonating urgency.

"Batch C is drifting," a junior researcher said from a neighboring station.

"Adjust the CO₂ by point two and re-run the calibration," Grace said. "Log the variance and flag the last stable checkpoint."

The junior nodded and moved with the precise panic of someone who cared too much to show it.

Grace took a sip of coffee that had taken on the flavor of perseverance. A photo sat propped near the edge of her desk: three faces in winter coats, cheeks bright from cold; David's arm around two small girls; a scarf she still owned. She straightened the frame as if it could anchor the table.

Her phone buzzed with a calendar reminder: Call Kate. 8:30. The notification wore its lateness without shame. 11:14 blinked back from the corner of the screen.

"Run the overnight?" Dr. Han said from the doorway, tie loosened, eyes kind behind end-of-day.

"We have a window on funding if we hit reproducibility this week," Grace said. "I'd like to catch it before it shuts."

"Go home for four hours," Dr. Han said. "Then come back and catch it."

"I will," Grace said. [I won't.] She set the mug down and leaned into the keyboard. The code on the middle monitor stopped complaining and began to assemble a graph that behaved.

The phone buzzed again—this time not a reminder but a text from Sofia: You missed dinner. Left you soup. Also Suzan says the darkroom is off-limits forever. xo

Grace typed: Thank you. Tell her I'm proud of the boundaries. She hovered on the send button. [Call Kate now. Hear her voice.]

The junior researcher cleared a throat. "The drift is corrected."

"Good," Grace said without looking away. She hit send on the text instead of the call.

The countdown clock rolled to 11:15. On the timelapse, the cells did what they were asked to do. In the quiet, Grace made a small, unrepeatable sound that lived in the narrow space between relief and regret.

She looked at the photo of winter and touched the glass with two fingers. "One more hour," Grace said to the room. [Make it worth what you miss.] She opened a new log, named it with today's date, and kept working while the lab's artificial morning pretended to rise.
